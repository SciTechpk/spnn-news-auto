UN chief praises Pakistan’s peace initiatives to ‘reduce tensions and create space for dialogue’

UNITED NATIONS: UN Secretary-General Antonio Guterres on Thursday praised Pakistan’s initiative at the UN Security Council (UNSC) to strengthen mechanisms for the peaceful settlement of disputes, saying the move was particularly important at a time when conflicts were escalating across the Middle East and beyond. Addressing an UNSC open debate held pursuant to Pakistan-sponsored Resolution 2788 (2025), the UN chief presented recommendations aimed at making diplomacy, mediation and conflict prevention more effective tools for maintaining international peace and security. The resolution, which was presented by Pakistan during its presidency of the UNSC last year and adopted unanimously by the Council, seeks to strengthen the use of peaceful means available under the UN Charter, including negotiation, mediation, conciliation, arbitration and judicial settlement. Pakistan had argued that diplomacy should not be used only after conflicts erupt but should serve as a means to prevent crises from escalating. “As conflicts escalate across the Middle East and beyond, military solutions cannot deliver lasting peace,” Guterres said, calling for restraint, protection of civilians and renewed commitment to diplomacy. “The situation is getting out of control. It’s teetering on the edge of the unimaginable,” the secretary-general warned, noting that the region was being “pulled into another widening circle of confrontation.” “One crisis feeds another. One escalation triggers the next,” he said, adding that as this dynamic spreads, “the political objectives are becoming increasingly obscured by the confrontation itself.” “It’s time to step back,” Guterres said, stressing that “attacks on civilian infrastructure are unacceptable” and that “there’s no military solution to the conflict.” “Fighting must stop everywhere in the region,” he said, while calling for the restoration of “international navigational rights and freedoms in and around the Strait of Hormuz and Bab al-Mandab.” “Diplomacy is the only way forward,” the UN chief added. Recognising Pakistan’s diplomatic efforts, Guterres said: “I want to recognise the mediation of Pakistan and countries in the region working to reduce tensions and create space for dialogue.” “These efforts must be supported and strengthened in pursuit of a peaceful and durable settlement,” he said. The secretary-general also highlighted developments in the occupied West Bank, saying: “We see creeping annexation with violence, settlement expansion, extensive Israeli military operations and large-scale displacement.” Pakistan welcomed the secretary-general’s recommendations, with Ambassador Asim Iftikhar Ahmad, Islamabad’s permanent representative to the UN, expressing “profound appreciation” for what he described as “thoughtful, practical and forward-looking” proposals. Speaking at the UNSC debate, Ambassador Ahmad said sustained implementation of the recommendations would strengthen not only mechanisms for peaceful settlement of disputes but also reinforce implementation of the UN Charter itself. “These recommendations carry significance far beyond today’s meeting,” he said, adding that they could help “restore conflict prevention and resolution to its rightful place, at the heart of the United Nations, and translate the promise of Chapter VI into practice.” The ambassador said Resolution 2788 was conceived in the spirit that diplomacy must not be “the instrument we scramble for, after peace has failed,” but rather the means through which failure could be prevented. He said the challenge was not the absence of mechanisms under the Charter but their “delayed, selective and inconsistent use,” warning that the Security Council often became engaged only after positions had hardened and the space for compromise had narrowed. Ambassador Ahmad said Pakistan remained committed to peaceful settlement of disputes and cited Islamabad’s recent diplomatic engagement in West Asia as an example of efforts to facilitate dialogue. He said Pakistan, together with partner countries, had played a constructive mediating role between Iran and the United States, helping create space for negotiations. He expressed regret over renewed hostilities and urged all sides to exercise restraint and return to dialogue, saying that whenever negotiations falter, “the answer is not in less but more determined diplomacy.” The Pakistani envoy called for stronger follow-up mechanisms for UNSC resolutions, greater use of fact-finding and mediation tools, increased support for the secretary-general’s good offices, and closer cooperation between the UN and regional organisations. He said the value of Resolution 2788 would ultimately be measured not by the unanimity with which it was adopted but by the consistency with which it was implemented. “History documents the wars that broke out. It rarely records the war that did not begin because a channel remained open, a mediator persisted, a leader exercised restraint or an institution acted in time,” Ambassador Ahmad said, describing prevention and peaceful settlement as diplomacy’s highest achievements.

What are the main bases housing US forces in the Middle East?

During its war with the United States, Iran has targeted Middle East bases where American troops are stationed, turning assets once seen as a bulwark against Tehran into targets. Below, AFP examines some of the major concentrations of US forces in the region, which fall under the US military’s Central Command (Centcom). Elizabeth Dent, a senior fellow at the Washington Institute for Near East Policy, said Middle East bases housing US troops “can support a wide range of missions from logistics, pre-positioning assets, refuelling, and of course combat operations”. Iran, in justifying its own retaliatory strikes, has accused Gulf states of allowing the US to use the bases to launch attacks, which Dent said was likely, though Tehran’s “perennial argument that all of these bases are inherently offensive is not accurate”. Bahrain The small kingdom hosts the US Navy’s Fifth Fleet and US Naval Forces Central Command headquarters. Bahrain’s deep-water port can accommodate the largest US military vessels, such as aircraft carriers, and the US Navy has used the base since 1948, when it was operated by Britain’s Royal Navy. US ships including anti-mine vessels and logistical support ships have their home port in Bahrain. Bahrain has faced heavy Iranian strikes, and early in the war, Manama briefly evacuated the neighborhood hosting the Fifth Fleet as it came under attack. Iraq Iraq is a close Iran ally, but also a strategic US partner. American troops are deployed as part of the US-led coalition to fight the Islamic State group in Iraq’s northern Kurdistan region, mostly at the Erbil airport and al-Harir air base. The coalition is preparing to end its mission in Iraq by the end of September. Iraqi groups have hit US facilities, including diplomatic missions in Baghdad and Erbil, more than 600 times during the war. Last week, a US service member was killed in northern Iraq during the controlled detonation of unexploded ordnance from a downed Iranian drone. Jordan While Iran often refers to “American bases” in Jordan, Amman and Washington maintain that the sites are Jordanian bases housing US troops. In the run-up to the February 28 strikes on Iran that launched the war, the US reportedly deployed dozens of military aircraft to Jordan’s Muwaffaq Salti Air Base, which also houses other Western forces. The base has F-16, F-15 and F-35 fighter jets, as well as refuelling and reconnaissance aircraft, drones, and Patriot and THAAD air defense systems. Iranian attacks recently killed three US service members in Jordan. “In recent years, largely because of Iran’s growing missile, drone, and proxy network, the US shifted toward more nimble basing along the western side of the Middle East region” in Israel and Jordan, according to Dent, a former director for the Gulf and Arabian Peninsula in the Office of the US Secretary of Defence. Kuwait The US has had a significant military presence in Kuwait after coming to its aid when Iraq invaded in 1990. It has several US bases, including Camp Arifjan, housing the forward headquarters for the US Army component of Centcom. Ali al-Salem Air Base hosts the 386th Air Expeditionary Wing, which serves as a primary airlift hub and gateway to deliver combat power to joint and coalition forces in the region. Washington has also deployed drones including MQ-9 Reapers to Kuwait. Six US service members have been killed there since the war began, as well as four Kuwaiti troops and border guards. Qatar Qatar houses the Al Udeid Air Base, which includes forward components of Centcom, as well the command’s air forces and special operations forces. The largest US base in the Middle East, it hosts rotating combat aircraft and the 379th Air Expeditionary Wing, which includes airlift, aerial refuelling, intelligence, surveillance, reconnaissance and medical evacuation assets. Iran hit Al Udeid during the current war, having first attacked it in June 2025 following US strikes targeting Iranian nuclear facilities. United Arab Emirates The UAE’s Al Dhafra Air Base hosts the US 380th Air Expeditionary Wing, a force composed of 10 squadrons of aircraft that also includes drones such as MQ-9 Reapers. Combat aircraft have rotated through Al Dhafra, which also hosts the US Air Force’s hub for advanced air warfare training. Saudi Arabia US forces returned to Saudi Arabia in 2019 following a bilateral agreement. The kingdom does not have American bases but it hosts troops at its Prince Sultan Air Base in Al-Kharj, outside Riyadh, which houses the 378th Air Expeditionary Wing and has come under attack. A US service member in Saudi Arabia was killed after sustaining wounds in an Iranian attack in March. Despite deadly attacks, Chatham House Middle East expert Neil Quilliam said he did not expect Gulf states to contemplate reducing the US military presence, “though DC itself, is likely to reconsider its military posture in the region”.

April 2011 Anti-corruption activist Anna Hazare started the first of two hunger strikes in a bid to push the government to bring in anti-graft legislation. Hundreds of thousands rallied to his support across the country and he was briefly jailed by the then-Congress alliance government. India’s parliament eventually passed an anti-graft law, though Hazare said it watered down his demands. Some of his supporters went on to form the Aam Aadmi Party in 2012, now one of Modi’s opponents in parliament. December 2012 Public fury over the gang-rape of a young woman on a moving bus in New Delhi erupted into mass protests in the capital.
